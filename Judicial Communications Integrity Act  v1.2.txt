Judicial Communications Integrity Act
SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the “Judicial Communications Integrity Act.”
SECTION 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSE.
(a) Findings.
Congress finds the following:
1. The independence of the federal judiciary is essential to the constitutional structure of the United States.
2. Federal courts exercise constitutional authority through judgments, orders, opinions, and other lawful acts that must remain available for public understanding, compliance, review, appeal, and correction through lawful process.
3. Federal executive agencies speak with official authority when they issue, authorize, adopt, or disseminate public communications through official agency channels, official titles, official accounts, official seals, official websites, or other indicia of agency authority.
4. Official agency communications concerning federal judicial rulings perform a public-record, compliance, and institutional-coordination function. Such communications may describe a ruling, state an agency’s obligations, identify lawful next steps, explain legal disagreement, announce an intent to seek review, and communicate operational consequences.
5. Good-faith legal criticism of judicial rulings is lawful and important. Nothing in this Act prohibits federal executive agencies from explaining why they believe a judicial ruling is erroneous, overbroad, unsupported by precedent, inconsistent with statute, inconsistent with the Constitution, or otherwise subject to lawful challenge.
6. The constitutional problem addressed by this Act is narrower: official agency communications that, without record basis, personally attack a federal judge, falsely attribute improper motive, materially misstate judicial authority, or characterize a lawful judicial ruling in a manner that undermines public compliance with court orders rather than preserving lawful disagreement through recognized channels.
7. Public confidence in courts and the judiciary has declined sharply in recent years. Congress has a legitimate institutional interest in protecting the structural independence of the judiciary, preserving public compliance with lawful judicial orders, and ensuring that official executive-branch communications concerning federal courts remain accurate, record-tethered, and institutionally responsible.
8. Congress has authority to regulate the internal operations, records, reporting duties, employment standards, and official communications practices of federal executive agencies, subject to constitutional limits.
9. This Act regulates official agency communications only. It does not regulate private-capacity speech, campaign speech, academic commentary, journalistic commentary, political commentary outside official agency channels, statements in court filings, or lawful public debate concerning judicial philosophy, constitutional interpretation, statutory policy, or public law.
10. A correction without record continuity is not repair. For official agency communications, repair requires changed future carry: correction, preservation of the record, agency learning, and standards that guide later official communications.
(b) Purpose.
The purposes of this Act are:
1. to protect judicial independence and public compliance with lawful judicial orders;
2. to preserve lawful executive-branch disagreement with judicial rulings through motions, appeals, briefing, public legal explanation, and other recognized channels;
3. to require official agency communications concerning federal judicial rulings to remain accurate, record-tethered, nonpersonal, and institutionally responsible;
4. to ensure that improper official agency communications are corrected without erasing the public record; and
5. to create an internal executive-agency communications standard that preserves both constitutional accountability and lawful public criticism.
SECTION 3. DEFINITIONS.
For the purposes of this Act:
1. Federal executive agency means an executive agency as defined in 5 U.S.C. § 105. The term does not include Congress, the federal judiciary, state or local governments, private persons, private organizations, or federal employees acting solely in a private capacity.
2. Official agency communication means any public-facing statement, press release, social media post, written communication, video statement, digital publication, guidance document, or other communication that is issued, authorized, adopted, approved, posted, reposted, quoted with approval, or disseminated by a federal executive agency, or by an officer or employee who possesses actual authority to speak on behalf of the agency and purports to exercise that authority.
3. Indicia of official agency authority include use of an agency name, agency seal, agency letterhead, agency website, official government account, official government email address, official title, official press office, official spokesperson, or any other context reasonably indicating that the communication is agency speech rather than private-capacity expression.
4. Agency adoption means that a federal executive agency has made a statement its own by publishing it through an official agency channel, incorporating it into an official agency communication, directing agency personnel to distribute it, or otherwise presenting it as the position or communication of the agency.
5. Federal judge means a justice, judge, magistrate judge, bankruptcy judge, or other judicial officer of an Article III court or federal court created by Act of Congress.
6. Judicial ruling means a judgment, order, opinion, injunction, stay, writ, mandate, or other lawful act of a federal court or federal judge in a case, controversy, proceeding, or matter within that court’s jurisdiction.
7. Covered judicial communication means an official agency communication concerning a federal judge, federal court, or judicial ruling.
8. Prohibited denigrating statement means a covered judicial communication that does one or more of the following without a reasonable basis in the judicial record, a court filing, a judicial finding, or another specific and verifiable public record:
(A) falsely attributes corruption, treason, criminality, personal bad faith, improper political motive, or improper ideological motive to a federal judge because of a judicial ruling;
(B) uses a personalized derogatory epithet or stock personal label, including “corrupt judge,” “traitor judge,” “enemy judge,” “lawless judge,” or substantially equivalent terminology, as an official characterization of a federal judge rather than as part of record-based legal criticism;
(C) asserts that a federal judge or judicial ruling has endangered the public, aided an enemy, obstructed lawful governance, or caused imminent harm without identifying the specific facts, operational consequences, or record materials supporting that assertion;
(D) materially misstates the content, effect, jurisdictional basis, procedural posture, or binding force of a judicial ruling in a manner likely to impair public understanding of legal obligations or public compliance with a court order; or
(E) states or implies that federal executive agencies may disregard, evade, or treat as nonbinding a lawful judicial ruling except through lawful process, including stay, appeal, modification, rehearing, or other recognized legal procedure.
9. Good-faith legal criticism means a covered judicial communication that identifies legal, constitutional, statutory, procedural, evidentiary, jurisdictional, remedial, operational, or policy grounds for disagreement with a judicial ruling without making a prohibited denigrating statement.
10. Emergency public-safety communication means a covered judicial communication issued because an agency reasonably believes immediate public notice is necessary to address an imminent threat to life, bodily safety, national security, or critical government operations. Such communication must be limited to specific facts, operational consequences, compliance posture, and lawful next steps.
11. Personal-capacity communication means speech by an officer or employee that is not issued, authorized, adopted, or disseminated as official agency speech and that does not purport to exercise actual authority to speak on behalf of a federal executive agency.
12. Corrective communication means a public agency communication that identifies, corrects, clarifies, withdraws, or supersedes a prior official agency communication while preserving the public record of what was corrected and why.
SECTION 4. OFFICIAL AGENCY COMMUNICATIONS STANDARD. 
(a) General Rule.
A federal executive agency shall not issue, authorize, adopt, approve, post, repost, quote with approval, or disseminate an official agency communication that contains a prohibited denigrating statement concerning a federal judge, federal court, or judicial ruling. 
(b) Internal Communications Standard. 
Each covered judicial communication shall, to the extent applicable: 
   1. accurately identify the judicial ruling being discussed;
   2. distinguish the agency’s legal disagreement from the agency’s obligation to comply unless and until lawful relief is obtained;
   3. identify any lawful next step the agency intends to pursue, including stay, appeal, rehearing, modification, clarification, or other recognized legal process;
   4. avoid personal attack on any federal judge; and
   5. avoid material misstatement of the ruling, the record, the court’s authority, or the agency’s compliance posture.
(c) Lawful Disagreement Preserved. 
Nothing in this Act prohibits an agency from stating that it disagrees with a judicial ruling, believes a judicial ruling is legally erroneous, believes a judicial ruling is unconstitutional, believes a judicial ruling exceeds statutory authority, believes a judicial ruling creates operational difficulty, or intends to seek lawful review.
(d) Public Explanation Preserved.
Nothing in this Act requires agency disagreement with a judicial ruling to occur only in court filings. Agencies may issue public explanations of their legal position, policy position, operational concerns, compliance obligations, and intended next steps, provided that such communications do not contain prohibited denigrating statements.
(e) Emergency Communications.
An emergency public-safety communication may be issued when immediate notice is necessary to address an imminent threat to life, bodily safety, national security, or critical government operations. Such communication shall be limited to specific facts, operational consequences, compliance posture, and lawful next steps. The agency shall conduct an after-action review under section 6(c)(4).
(f) Viewpoint Neutral Application.
This Act applies without regard to the political, ideological, jurisprudential, policy, or litigation viewpoint of the agency, the administration, the federal judge, the judicial ruling, or any party to the litigation.
(g) No Agency Adoption of Private Denigration.
A federal executive agency shall not avoid this Act by adopting, reposting, quoting with approval, embedding, linking as endorsement, or otherwise disseminating a private-capacity statement as official agency communication if that statement would violate this Act when issued directly by the agency.
SECTION 5. AGENCY IMPLEMENTATION. 
(a) Written Standards.
Not later than 90 days after enactment, each federal executive agency shall adopt written standards implementing this Act.
(b) Required Contents.
The written standards shall require procedures for:
   1. identifying covered judicial communications before publication;
   2. confirming the accuracy of descriptions of judicial rulings;
   3. distinguishing legal disagreement from compliance obligation;
   4. identifying lawful next steps when an agency intends to seek review;
   5. avoiding prohibited denigrating statements;
   6. distinguishing official agency communications from personal-capacity communications;
   7. correcting improper covered judicial communications without erasing the public record;
   8. preserving records of covered judicial communications and corrective communications; and
   9. training employees who draft, approve, post, or disseminate official agency communications.
(c) Pre-Publication Review.
Each agency shall establish a reasonable internal review procedure for covered judicial communications that present a substantial risk of noncompliance with this Act. The procedure may be expedited when necessary for emergency public-safety communications.
(d) Correction Procedure.
Each agency shall establish a correction procedure that allows the agency to issue a corrective communication, archive or label the prior communication as corrected, and preserve sufficient records to show what was corrected, when, by whom, and why.
SECTION 6. REVIEW, CORRECTION, AND OVERSIGHT. 
(a) Inspector General Review.
An alleged violation of this Act shall be referred to the Inspector General of the relevant agency for review. The Inspector General may dismiss a complaint that does not present prima facie evidence of a covered judicial communication containing a prohibited denigrating statement. 
(b) Timelines.
The Inspector General shall make an initial triage determination within 10 days of referral and shall complete any investigation within 60 days unless the Inspector General determines in writing that additional time is necessary. 
(c) Corrective Action.
If the Inspector General determines that a violation occurred, the Inspector General may recommend one or more of the following:
   1. issuance of a corrective communication;
   2. archival labeling of the prior communication as corrected, withdrawn, superseded, or noncompliant;
   3. removal of the communication from active official channels while preserving records required by law;
   4. employee training or revision of agency communications procedures;
   5. appropriate discipline consistent with federal employment law; or
   6. any other corrective action consistent with this Act and applicable law.
(d) Record Preservation.
An agency shall not treat correction as erasure. The agency shall preserve records sufficient to identify the original communication, the correction, the reason for correction, and the official responsible for issuing or authorizing the corrective communication.
(e) Judicial Branch Notice.
If an investigation is opened concerning a covered judicial communication directed at a pending matter or at a federal judge in relation to a pending matter, notice shall be provided in a manner that avoids improper ex parte communication. Such notice may be provided to the Administrative Office of the United States Courts, the clerk of the relevant court, or another appropriate judicial administrative office rather than directly to the presiding judge.
(f) Annual Reports.
Each Inspector General shall submit an annual report to Congress summarizing the number of complaints, dismissals, investigations, findings, corrective communications, procedural revisions, and trends under this Act.
(g) Emergency After-Action Review.
When an agency issues an emergency public-safety communication under section 4(e), the Inspector General shall conduct an after-action review within 15 days to determine whether the communication complied with this Act and whether corrective communication is required.
(h) Whistleblower Protection.
An employee who reports an alleged violation in good faith shall be protected from retaliation to the extent provided by 5 U.S.C. § 2302 and other applicable law.
(i) No Private Right of Action.
Nothing in this Act creates a private right of action for damages, injunctive relief, declaratory relief, attorney’s fees, suppression of evidence, vacatur of agency action, judicial recusal, contempt, or alteration of any judicial ruling.
SECTION 7. RULE OF CONSTRUCTION AND CONSTITUTIONAL BOUNDARIES. 
Nothing in this Act shall be interpreted to:
   1. restrict the personal First Amendment rights of federal officers or employees acting in a private capacity;
   2. restrict campaign speech, political-party speech, academic commentary, journalistic commentary, civic commentary, or other speech outside official agency communication;
   3. restrict statements made in court filings, administrative filings, sworn testimony, legal briefs, motions, notices of appeal, petitions, oral argument, settlement communications, or other legal proceedings;
   4. prohibit good-faith legal criticism of any judicial ruling;
   5. prohibit an agency from stating that it disagrees with a judicial ruling or intends to seek lawful review;
   6. require an agency to express approval of any judicial ruling;
   7. authorize any court to alter the merits, jurisdiction, remedy, timing, or enforceability of any judicial ruling because of an alleged violation of this Act;
   8. create any private right of action;
   9. regulate the President, Vice President, Members of Congress, judges, private persons, or state or local officials, except to the extent a federal executive agency adopts or disseminates a statement as official agency communication;
   10. limit lawful public debate regarding judicial philosophy, constitutional interpretation, statutory meaning, public policy, court reform, judicial selection, or litigation strategy; or
   11. displace any stricter lawful ethics, records, employment, communications, or professional-responsibility standard otherwise applicable to federal officers or employees.
SECTION 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.
This Act shall take effect 180 days after enactment.
SECTION 9. SEVERABILITY.
If any provision of this Act, or the application thereof to any person or circumstance, is held invalid, the remainder of this Act, and the application of such provision to other persons or circumstances, shall not be affected thereby.
